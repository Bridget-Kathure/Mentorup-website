Born a crime chapter 1 and 2

Trevor Noah talks about his upbringing in South Africa during the apartheid era,
where he was born to a black Xhosa mother and a Swiss father in 1984.
This led to a unique and challenging upbringing for Trevor during apartheid.
South Africa was divided into tribes, with the Xhosa and Zulu being the dominant tribes before apartheid.
The Zulu warriors fought against Europeans, while the Xhosa learned from them.
The colonizers used the tribe differences to turn them against each other to conquer them.
In 1927, an Immorality Act was passed that outlawed native black-and-white relationships,
with a punishment of not less than 5 years for men and 4 for women.
In the first chapter, Trevor describes his mother as a strong, independent, and staunch Christian.
We also see some of the violence in post-apartheid South Africa and the dangers
of the triumph of democracy over the apartheid situation.
Trevor’s mom throws him off a moving bus to save their lives. In their lives, they are constantly fleeing
either from the police officer looking to expose Trevor for being mixed race or Trevor from his mom to escape beating.
There were entire police squads whose sole responsibility was to peep through windows,
a task for only the best law enforcement officers. If they found any sexual relationship between a black and a white,
which was against the law, they would charge rape for a black man having intercourse with a white woman.
Trevor’s mother never let discrimination hold her back. She had an apartment, went out with friends,
and even dated her white neighbor, Robert, who would become Trevor's father.
Patricia, Trevor's mother, was determined to make her way in the world and had to overcome significant obstacles to do so.
She worked hard, taking a typing course to secure a job outside of the factory, and even lived in a part of town where she faced many challenges.
Despite these difficulties, Patricia expressed her desire to have a child to Trevor's father, a Swiss man, who agreed to her request.
In 1984, Patricia gave birth to a mixed-race child.
She defended Robert by claiming Trevor's father was from the adjacent kingdom of "Swaziland".
She also returned to see her family after three years apart, and Trevor spent some time with them.
Trevor was frequently kept indoors to avoid being spotted, endangering the entire family's safety.
If he had been discovered, his grandma and mother would most certainly have been arrested.
The chapters relate to the recent society by highlighting the themes of social injustices and racial inequalities.
Trevor’s experiences serve as a reminder of the struggles and progress in society’s journey toward equality and acceptance.
Trevor's experiences highlight the importance of family in life and how it shapes our attitudes and behaviors.
